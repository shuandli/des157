I approached Animate.css with the mindset of how it could help with my work's aesthetics. In other words, I wanted to see how it could make the graphics more visually engaging. The outcome was what I wanted, with the animations working as expected. However, Animate.css comes with numerous caveats that make it difficult to make sure everything is valid. Animate.css is a stylesheet I need to link to. However, the css file off the library puts up a ton of errors on the W3 validator, well over many several dozens to be exact. Also, linking to the Animate.css means my html stylization needs to be inline, so please do not think I suddenly got lazy while coding. On top of that, linking to JavaScript becomes very confusing. I am not enitrely sure if this is a library I will use in my final, but it does at least make for fun visuals. Link to Animate.css here: https://daneden.github.io/animate.css/

My Studio 3 most noticably connects to UI design through its top-down interaction. In other, words, the interaction is meant to start from the top and end up at the bottom, starting at selecting the hat and working down towards the sketchbook. The eye has an easy path to follow. 1/31/17....

National Geographic's hierarchy is apparent with the sizes and arrangements of its homepage photos. Each main photo covers the width of the page immediately drawing attention and is followed by two smaller photos below. They all have captions that are easy to read but small enough to not compete with the photos. Text is clear, giving it clarity and it repeats its visual hierarchy throughout. National Geographic's homepage is overall very easy to follow. Interaction reference.

Facebook, a website I visit all too much. The fact that I find it easy to use means I am either very used to it or it is that easy to use. Breaking it down, there is quite a bit of intuitive design. The two center columns showing the newsfeed and news/ad space are the largest, so it's what my eyes go to first, as users would want to immediately read what's going on. The chat bar and group information off to either side are easily distinguishable with multiple icons and colors with the search bar up top like on a typical browser window. Facebook's homepage is overall sectioned off and easy to navigate.
